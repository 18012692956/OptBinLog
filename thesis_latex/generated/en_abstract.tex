For embedded and edge systems, logging must balance observability with overhead (storage, CPU, and concurrent initialization). This thesis implements Optbinlog, a schema-shared binary logging approach for embedded deployment. Event metadata are parsed once, materialized in shared memory, and accessed via offsets, so runtime hot-path cost is reduced and cross-process mapping remains stable.

The work first surveys representative domestic and international logging systems (Linux ftrace, LTTng, Zephyr logging, systemd-journald, NanoLog, RT-Thread ulog, OpenHarmony HiLog Lite) and analyzes their optimization code paths. Then a theory-first model is built to derive how time/space/throughput scale with log count \(N\) and device count \(D\). Finally, measured results are compared against the derived trends.

This thesis emphasizes the latest strict-aligned evidence: single-node tests, local multi-device scans (\(D=5,10,20,50\)), real multi-node runs (5/10/15/20 nodes with hardware CRC32C), and space crossover scans (\thesisterm{N=1...100000}). \thesisterm{binary} consistently outperforms \thesisterm{text} across all included semantic groups. Under native-path fairness (Optbinlog aligned to peer semantics while peers keep their original storage paths), \thesisterm{binary} remains clearly ahead of \thesisterm{syslog/ulog} in real multi-node runs, stays mixed against \thesisterm{hilog}, and still trails \thesisterm{nanolog/zephyr} in node-level comparisons; \thesisterm{ftrace} keeps a split pattern (space-positive but time/throughput-negative). Space scans show crossover near \(N\approx 10\) for \thesisterm{syslog/ulog}, near \(N\approx 50\) for \thesisterm{hilog}, and no observed crossover for \thesisterm{nanolog/zephyr} up to \(N=100000\). Initialization-race results consistently satisfy the single-creator property. Overall, theoretical predictions and empirical trends are aligned.
