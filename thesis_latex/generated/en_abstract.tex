Embedded and edge logging systems must provide observability under tight constraints on computation, storage, and concurrent access. Although text-based logging is easy to read and widely compatible, it incurs high formatting overhead, substantial storage redundancy, and repeated metadata parsing in high-frequency workloads. When multiple processes or nodes share the same event description, initialization races and consistency risks further complicate deployment. To address these issues, this thesis designs and implements Optbinlog, an embedded binary logging system built on shared event schemas.

The study follows a mechanism-model-evidence workflow. First, Optbinlog is designed with shared schemas, offset-based access, self-describing frames, and per-record integrity checks so that schema parsing is moved out of the steady-state hot path while cross-process consistency is preserved. Second, analytical models are developed to characterize time, space, and throughput as functions of log count \(N\) and concurrent device count \(D\), with explicit separation of fixed costs, marginal costs, and contention terms. Third, the design is evaluated under unified statistical rules and strict semantic alignment across single-node, local multi-device, real multi-node proxy, and log-volume scaling scenarios.

The results show that Optbinlog consistently improves over text-based paths in both time and space. Under strict fairness, it delivers stable overall advantages against \thesisterm{syslog/ulog}, still leaves room for reducing per-record constant overhead when compared with ultra-short-path peers such as \thesisterm{nanolog/zephyr}, and shows a mixed profile against \thesisterm{hilog} where space remains favorable while time/throughput become scenario-sensitive under node-level contention. Space-crossover experiments further indicate that Optbinlog can release storage benefits early when compared with \thesisterm{syslog/ulog}, and can also surpass \thesisterm{hilog} beyond a moderate log volume. Initialization-race experiments validate the single-creator reuse semantics of the shared schema file in both multi-process and node-level concurrent settings. Overall, the empirical trends agree well with the theoretical analysis, indicating that the proposed shared-schema mechanism is well suited to embedded deployments that value cross-process consistency, structured semantics, and long-running online operation.
